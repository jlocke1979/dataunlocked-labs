# Life Flow — Visual Style Guide

## 1. Project Overview

Life Flow is an interactive narrative visualization exploring the U.S. organ transplant system. The project uses data storytelling to help viewers understand the scale, flow, constraints, and human stakes of organ transplantation.

The intended tone is respectful, calm, and data-driven. The visualization should create emotional resonance without overwhelming the viewer. The goal is to balance human impact with analytical clarity.

Primary audience:
- General viewers who may know little about the transplant system
- Potential organ donors
- Viewers interested in public health, systems, and data storytelling

Primary goals:
- Explain how the transplant system functions at a high level
- Show the gap between organ need and transplant availability
- Communicate the human consequences of delay and scarcity
- Encourage awareness and donor registration

Primary data sources are expected to include:
- United Network for Organ Sharing (UNOS)
- Organ Procurement and Transplantation Network (OPTN)
- Scientific Registry of Transplant Recipients (SRTR)
- HRSA transplant statistics and reports

The project prioritizes authoritative and publicly available healthcare data sources to support clarity, credibility, and ethical communication.


---

## 2. Design Philosophy

Life Flow should feel like a human-centered systems story, not a dashboard.

The visual style should emphasize:
- Clarity over spectacle
- Emotional restraint
- Calm pacing
- Ethical representation of sensitive health data
- Minimal visual clutter
- Smooth continuity between scenes
- A sense of motion, flow, and connection

The design should avoid sensationalizing illness, death, or scarcity. The subject matter is serious enough on its own. The role of the visualization is to help viewers understand the system with dignity and focus.

The visualization should communicate complexity without becoming visually overwhelming. The transplant system is presented as a living network of flow, delay, scarcity, and coordination rather than as isolated statistics or disconnected charts.

The visual metaphor of flow is central to the project identity:
- movement suggests life and continuity
- bottlenecks suggest scarcity and delay
- fading suggests loss or unresolved outcomes
- restored movement suggests successful transplant and renewed possibility


---

## 3. Narrative Structure

The show follows a structured narrative arc:

1. Landing — Entry and orientation
2. Situation — A national system connects thousands of lives
3. System — The transplant process has structure, logic, and flow
4. Tension — Not every path completes
5. Problem — Waiting, delay, and loss accumulate
6. Resolution — Increased participation can improve outcomes
7. Outro — A personal call to awareness and action

Each scene should answer one primary question and introduce only as much information as the viewer needs at that point in the story.

- Gray/sage tones represent waiting or unresolved status.
- Green represents successful transplant or restored flow.
- Red/burgundy represents loss or death while waiting.
- Teal represents process, movement, or transition.
- Accent colors should be used only for emphasis.

## 5. Typography System

The typography should be simple, readable, and restrained.

Recommended system:
- Headings: a calm serif or elegant display font
- Body/UI text: a clean sans-serif
- Labels and numeric values: clean sans-serif, medium weight

Typography goals:
- Maintain readability
- Avoid decorative fonts
- Support a serious public-health tone
- Create hierarchy without visual noise

Suggested pairings:
- Libre Baskerville + Inter
- Merriweather + Inter
- Georgia + Arial / Helvetica

Final font choices may be refined later, but the system should remain simple: one expressive heading font and one readable interface font.

## 6. Motion and Animation Principles

Motion is central to the identity of Life Flow.

Animation should communicate:
- Flow
- Delay
- Connection
- Bottlenecks
- Resolution
- Loss

Motion rules:
- Use slow, smooth transitions.
- Avoid chaotic or distracting movement unless representing system tension.
- Preserve continuity between scenes when possible.
- Dots or particles should feel persistent rather than constantly recreated.
- Motion should reveal meaning, not decorate the screen.
- Scene transitions should feel cinematic and calm.

---

## 7. Visual Grammar

The core visual metaphor is flow through a system.

Potential visual elements:
- Dots or particles represent people, organs, or transplant-related events depending on scene context.
- Lines, paths, or channels represent movement through the transplant system.
- Clustering represents accumulation or backlog.
- Narrowing channels represent bottlenecks.
- Fading represents loss, delay, or removal from the system.
- Brightening or greening represents successful transplant or restored flow.

Visual grammar rules:
- Each mark should have a clear meaning.
- Do not overload one visual element with too many meanings.
- Maintain consistent meanings across scenes.
- Use legends when visual encoding changes.
- Avoid decorative marks that do not support comprehension.

---

## 8. Layout Principles

The layout should feel spacious, calm, and focused.

Layout rules:
- Use generous whitespace.
- Keep text minimal on screen.
- Use short narrative titles.
- Avoid crowding legends, annotations, and chart elements.
- Keep legends consistent across related scenes.
- Use progressive disclosure: reveal one idea at a time.
- Maintain a clear focal point for each scene.
- Avoid forcing viewers to interpret too many encodings at once.

Annotations should be brief and purposeful. They should guide attention, not explain everything.

---

## 9. Accessibility Standards

Because this project concerns public health and human outcomes, accessibility is a core design requirement.

Accessibility principles:
- Use readable font sizes.
- Avoid relying on color alone.
- Use labels, position, opacity, or shape as supporting encodings.
- Maintain sufficient contrast for text.
- Avoid fast flashing or abrupt motion.
- Keep interactions simple and discoverable.
- Make navigation visible, not keyboard-only.

The visual system should remain understandable for users with limited subject knowledge or limited visualization literacy.

## 10. Scene-Level Style Notes

### Landing
Feeling: invited, calm, curious  
Visual approach: minimal title, subtle motion, low cognitive load

### Situation
Feeling: scale, importance, human connection  
Visual approach: establish the system and population scale

### System
Feeling: structure, logic, order  
Visual approach: reveal how donors, organs, patients, and outcomes connect

### Tension
Feeling: unease, imbalance  
Visual approach: stalled movement, partial connections, delay


### Problem
Feeling: weight, seriousness, systemic fragility  
Visual approach:
- backlog accumulation
- narrowing pathways
- stalled movement
- fading outcomes
- visible drop-offs throughout the system

Narrative emphasis:
The problem is not only donor shortage. Opportunities can be lost at multiple stages including donor eligibility, organ utilization, matching, transport timing, and hospital capacity.



### Resolution
Feeling: hope, possibility  
Visual approach: smoother flow, more successful connections, reduced blockage

### Outro
Feeling: reflective, personal, grounded  
Visual approach: simplified layout, call to action, minimal distraction

## 12. Conclusion

Life Flow’s visual identity is designed to make a complex transplant system understandable while preserving the dignity of the people represented by the data.

The style guide prioritizes clarity, restraint, consistency, and emotional honesty. The final show should help viewers understand that transplant outcomes depend not only on medical expertise, but also on systems, timing, participation, and public awareness.
